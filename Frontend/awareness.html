Sickle Cell Awareness

Sickle cell disease often remains unseen until a crisis occurs. Many children and adults living with the condition appear healthy most of the time, yet silently face risks that can become life-threatening without warning. Pain episodes, severe infections, and organ damage can develop suddenly, especially when the disease is not well managed. This is why early diagnosis and consistent care are essential. When sickle cell is identified early, complications can be prevented, controlled, or reduced significantly.

Sickle cell disease is a genetic condition passed from parents to their children. A child develops the disease when both parents carry the sickle cell trait. In Kenya, many people carry the trait without knowing it. Newborn screening, early testing, and knowing one’s sickle cell status help families make informed decisions and ensure timely medical care.

Children diagnosed early can receive lifesaving interventions, including routine check-ups, preventive medications, and vaccinations. Adolescents and adults benefit from regular follow-up, education on avoiding triggers, and prompt treatment when symptoms arise. Sickle cell care services are available in public hospitals and specialized clinics across the country, though awareness remains the first step to access.

Preventing severe sickle cell complications does not start in crisis.
It starts with knowing your status.

This Sickle Cell Awareness Month, let’s stand with those living with sickle cell, support early diagnosis, and choose informed action for healthier families and stronger communities.
